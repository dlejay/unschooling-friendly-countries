# Unschooling in Nigeria

## Compulsory education

The Compulsory, Free Universal Basic Education Act, 2004 (UBE Act) makes primary and junior secondary education compulsory.
Section 15 defines a "child or ward" as a person "between the age of 6 years and 16 years",
primary school as a six-year course for pupils aged six to twelve,
and junior secondary school as a three-year course for pupils aged twelve to fifteen.

Section 2(2) requires every parent to ensure that his child "attends and completes" primary and junior secondary education
"by endeavouring to send the child to primary and junior secondary schools",
subject to penalties (reprimand on first conviction, then fines of ₦2,000.00 and ₦5,000.00 or imprisonment of one to two months).
Section 4 goes further :

> **4.** — (1) Every parent shall ensure that his child receives full-time education suitable to his age, ability and aptitude by regular attendance at school.
>
> (2) The provisions of sections 2 (2) and 4 (1) of this Act shall not apply to any parent who, for the time being, is resident outside Nigeria.

Section 4(1) closely resembles the wording now found in section 7 of the English Education Act 1996,
but omits the words "or otherwise", which in England expressly allow education otherwise than at school.
The omission strongly indicates that, unlike in England, education otherwise than at school
is not an alternative means of discharging the Nigerian statutory duty.
Section 15 defines "school" as a primary or junior secondary school. The Act contains no mechanism
by which ordinary parent-directed instruction in a household may be recognised as regular attendance at such a school.

## Unschooling

The UBE Act does not establish home education as an alternative means of satisfying compulsory education.
Because section 4(1) requires full-time education "by regular attendance at school", and the only statutory exemption
(section 4(2)) concerns parents resident outside Nigeria, exclusively parent-directed education at home does not appear
to satisfy the federal statutory duty.

The Child Rights Act, 2003 (section 15) likewise requires every parent or guardian to ensure that the child attends and completes
primary and junior secondary education, without any home-education exemption.

Legislative and administrative responsibility for basic education is divided among federal, state and local authorities
(the UBE Act itself frames federal intervention as "an assistance to the States and Local Governments"),
and the detailed regulation of schools and enforcement of attendance duties varies by state.
However, no state statutory regime or generally available procedure has been identified
that gives parents a right to replace school attendance with ordinary parent-directed home education.

## Policy recognition

The National Policy on Non-State Schools in Nigeria (NPNSN 2024), approved by the National Council on Education in October 2024
and launched with its Implementation Guidelines by the Federal Government in July 2025
with support from the UK's FCDO-PLANE programme, expressly lists home schooling among the categories of non-state schools :
"Children, for various reasons, are taught at home by parents or paid tutors."
This appears to be the first known recognition of homeschooling in a general federal policy instrument.

The recognition does not, however, amount to a coherent homeschooling regime. The policy's generally applicable provisions require
non-state schools to register with the relevant education authority, implement the national curriculum,
use appropriately qualified and TRCN-licensed teachers, provide facilities such as classrooms, laboratories and libraries,
and submit to inspections and quality-assurance evaluations. These requirements were evidently drafted primarily
for institutional schools, and the policy provides no clear procedure explaining how they are to be adapted
to an individual household. Nor does a policy document amend the UBE Act : the statutory duty of regular school attendance remains.

State-level implementation of the non-state-schools framework is uneven and ongoing as of 2026 ;
no state procedure specifically authorising parent-run homeschooling has been identified.

The legal position is therefore as follows : homeschooling is now named in federal policy,
but there is no statutory exemption from school attendance and no clearly established registration route for families.
Home education therefore cannot be treated as legally open to all.
