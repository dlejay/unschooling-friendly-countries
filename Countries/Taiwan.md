# Unschooling in Taiwan

## Compulsory education

National education is compulsory from ages 6 to 15 — grades 1 to 9 — comprising six
years of primary school and three years of junior high school (國民教育法 ; 強迫入學條例).

## Context

As noted by homeschool.tw :

> Parents should apply no later than April 30 or October 31 each year to the city or county government of their residence by submitting an application form and a proposal outlining the objectives, method, and content (curriculum, assessment, facilities, and equipment used) of the education, as well as the expected outcomes and background information of the people involved in the child’s education.
>
> Parents may apply to do homeschooling for up to six years of elementary education, three years of junior secondary education, and three years of senior secondary education. The senior secondary education stage may be extended by two years if necessary.
>
> Applications are evaluated by the non-school education committee of each city or county, of which at least two fifths of the members must be non-school education parents or representatives of non-school education associations.
> Despite being homeschooled, elementary and junior secondary non-school students still need to be registered under a district school, which performs an administrative function. This includes accepting applications and issuing a graduation certificate once the non-school student has satisfied the graduation requirements as outlined in the application proposal and approved by the review committee.

The compulsory-attendance legislation (強迫入學條例) is framed around school-age
nationals (適齡國民). Secondary English-language sources therefore state that foreign
nationals may homeschool without restriction (HSLDA ; tw.forumosa.com), though
families may still choose to apply under the experimental-education regime if they
want local recognition or a clear administrative record.

Experimental education is not bound by the national curriculum guidelines. The
statutory plan (實驗教育計畫) must state its purpose, method, content (curriculum type,
teaching, and assessment) and expected outcomes, and is subject to review by the
relevant 審議會 (review committee).

## News

In mid-2023, Taiwan amended its education law to create — for the first time — a
legal basis for subsidising homeschooling (officially termed non-school-based
experimental education) at the elementary and junior-high levels.

On 29 May 2023, the Legislative Yuan passed an amendment to the Primary and Junior
High School Education Act (國民教育法). The revised Article 6 (2) provides that,
“to assist the development of … school-based experimental education and
non-school-based experimental education, the central competent authority shall
formulate associated subsidy regulations”.

The implementing regulation is the 教育部國民及學前教育署補助推動實驗教育要點
(Ministry of Education K-12 Administration Directions for Subsidising the Promotion
of Experimental Education). As amended on 21 October 2025, it does **not** establish
a universal subsidy at the compulsory-education stage. Under point 2 (4), at the
國民中小學 (K-9) stage, non-school-experimental-education student subsidies are
restricted to three categories : low-income households (低收入戶), lower-middle-income
households (中低收入戶), and students with disabilities (身心障礙學生).

The subsidy is also not a flat monthly stipend. Under points 3 (4) and 8 (5), it
reimburses the recurrent costs (經常門經費) of learning resources and counselling
resources matched to the student’s actual learning needs, disbursed through the
student’s registered (設籍) school against supporting documentation.

Before the 2023 amendment, only senior-high-stage homeschoolers received tuition
subsidies (about NT$20,000–30,000 per year) under the 12-year Basic Education
policy ; K-9 self-learners received none. The amendment created the legal basis to
extend support downward to the compulsory grades, but the scheme as implemented is
means- and category-tested rather than open to families of all socio-economic
backgrounds — the latter was the aim of the public petition that prompted the
change, not the policy ultimately adopted.
