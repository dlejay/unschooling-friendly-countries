# Unschooling in Nicaragua

## Compulsory education

Nicaraguan law is unclear on the age up to which education is compulsory.

The 2025 constitutional reform amended the Constitution’s education and culture provisions. The reformed text no longer contains an express constitutional rule making education compulsory. It states instead that education is free and of quality, that the State is responsible for planning, directing and organising it, and that the organisation, regulation and functioning of education are determined by law.

Under Article 19 of the General Education Law, basic education provided by the State is free and compulsory from the third level of initial education until the sixth grade of primary education, with gradual extension to later levels.

Article 23 places regular primary education between ages 6 and 12, and regular secondary education between ages 12 and 17. The General Education Law therefore clearly makes education compulsory at least up to the end of primary school / around age 12, while any extension beyond that is not clearly enacted in that law.

Article 43 of the Childhood and Adolescence Code, by contrast, states that both primary and secondary education are compulsory, suggesting a longer obligation, potentially up to the end of secondary education.

The U.S. Department of Labor calculates a compulsory education age of 12 based on available information, but notes that Nicaragua does not clearly establish a compulsory education age.

## Unschooling

Home education is not expressly regulated or affirmatively authorised.

Article 109 of the General Education Law recognises parents as the first persons responsible for their children’s education and gives them the duty and right to educate their children and freely decide the type and form of education they want for them. However, the same article also imposes an obligation to enrol children in educational institutions according to the parents’ preferences.

The General Education Law does not, in the passages reviewed, clearly impose a separate obligation of daily school attendance. Its clearest parental obligation is one of enrolment. This leaves room, at least in theory, for highly flexible or parent-led education around an institutional enrolment.

On the available sources, home education therefore appears to be a matter of practical tolerance and limited enforcement rather than a recognised legal route. Nicaragua can be treated as open in practice to home education or unschooling, provided the family is willing to manage the enrolment issue and accept the absence of a clear statutory homeschooling framework.

## News

In 2025, Nicaragua adopted a major constitutional reform, which amended the Constitution’s education and culture provisions.

The previous constitutional Article 121 stated that primary education was free and compulsory in State centres. That wording no longer appears in the reformed constitutional text, which now states more generally that education is free and of quality and that the State plans, directs and organises it.

The U.S. Department of Labor accordingly reports that Nicaragua still has no clear compulsory education age and that the 2025 constitution removed the previous mention of obligatory primary education.
