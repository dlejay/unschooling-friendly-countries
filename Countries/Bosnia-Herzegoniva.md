# Unschooling in Bosnia-Herzegovina

## Compulsory education

Education in Bosnia and Herzegovina is highly decentralised. State-level framework legislation sets common principles, but operative legislation and administration are handled by Republika Srpska, the ten cantons of the Federation of Bosnia and Herzegovina, and the Brčko District, subject to respecting the principles and standards of the Framework Law on Primary and Secondary Education in Bosnia and Herzegovina, which may not be reduced.

Under Article 16 of the Framework Law, elementary education is obligatory for all children. It begins in the calendar year in which the child reaches six years of age by 1 April and has lasted nine years since the 2004 reform. Secondary education is generally not compulsory, though local exceptions exist — notably Sarajevo Canton, where the first two years of secondary education are compulsory.

## Unschooling

According to the Framework Law :

> Article 12.
>
> All public primary schools shall have a catchment area. All students of compulsory school age living within that area shall have a right to enroll and attend this school. Attendance at this school is mandatory, unless the student is attending a private school or is exempted from attendance as further provided in the present Law.
>
> Upon request of the parent(s) of a child, the competent Ministry may in exceptional cases exempt the child from the requirement to attend the allocated school referred to in the present Article if this is necessary in order to protect the rights of the child and is found to be in the best interests of the child.

> Article 27.
>
> Parents are obliged to ensure regular attendance of their children in school during the period of obligatory education. In case of negligence and irresponsible behavior to this obligation, parents are subject to legal sanctions.

The exemption contemplated by Article 12 is an exemption from attendance at the allocated catchment school, not a general right to satisfy compulsory education outside school ; the provision continues to presuppose school enrolment, whether in a public school or a private school. Article 26 frames the parental right of choice as a right to educate one’s children in public and private schools. Independently of the Framework Law, the European Commission’s Eurydice network reports that Bosnia and Herzegovina provides no home-based education at any level of its system. The obligation applies to every child resident in the country and is enforced through the locally competent education authority ; where local law extends compulsory schooling, as in Sarajevo Canton, the compulsory period is longer.
