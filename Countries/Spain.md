# Unschooling in Spain

## Compulsory schooling

Spain mandates **10 years of compulsory schooling**, from age 6 to age 16, covering primary education (Educación Primaria, 6–12) and compulsory secondary education (Educación Secundaria Obligatoria / ESO, 12–16).
 
**Key legal sources :**
 
- **Constitution (1978), Article 27 :** guarantees the right to education, states basic education is compulsory and free, and recognises parental freedom to choose education in line with their convictions.
- **Organic Law on Education (LOE 2/2006), Sec. 4.2 :** shifts the language to compulsory *school attendance* (escolarización obligatoria) for all children aged 6–16.
- **LOMLOE (2020) :** the current governing education law (reform of LOE), retains the same compulsory attendance framework.
 
**The constitutional tension :** the Constitution mandates education ; the organic laws mandate schooling.
The Constitutional Court resolved this in **STC 133/2010** : current laws requiring school attendance are a lawful interpretation of the Constitution, but the Court explicitly acknowledged that a future law permitting homeschooling would also be constitutionally valid.
 
**What falls outside the compulsory window:**
 
- Pre-primary (Educación Infantil, 0–6): not compulsory.
The second cycle (3–6) is free and near-universal (~ 97.7 % participation in 2023) but attendance is voluntary.
- Post-16 (Bachillerato, FP) : entirely voluntary.
 
**Enforcement:** school attendance is monitored by schools, which report absenteeism to local education authorities and, if necessary, social services. Persistent non-attendance can trigger investigations for truancy or even criminal proceedings (abandono familiar). Outcomes vary by autonomous community — some regions are stricter than others.

## Recent development

- Amaya Cáceres, a homeschooling mother, was tried in 2024 for alleged "family abandonment" after 16 years of homeschooling her children ; the case ended in acquittal.

- Outcomes remain lottery-like : one lawyer recounts that a family who proactively notified authorities and documented their curriculum was left alone, while another family who didn't was reported, went to court, and had their children forced back into school.
