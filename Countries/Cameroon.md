# Unschooling in Cameroon

## Context

Cameroon’s education system is organised into two subsystems within a single national framework, reflecting the country’s colonial inheritance. The Anglophone subsystem, predominant in the Northwest and Southwest regions, is modelled on the British system, while the Francophone subsystem, used in the eight other regions, is modelled on the French system. The two retain distinct structures for cycles, assessment and certification, while the basic statutory framework governing compulsory education is national and common to both.

## Compulsory education

The _Loi d’orientation de l’éducation_ of 1998 provides :

> **Article 9**
>
> Primary education is compulsory.

## Unschooling

The _Loi d’orientation_ enumerates the settings in which education is delivered :

> **Article 23**
>
> 1. Education is provided in the following schools : nursery schools ; primary schools ; general secondary schools and high schools ; technical or vocational secondary schools and high schools ; post-primary schools ; teacher-training colleges for general and technical education.
> 2. It may also be provided through a distance-education system.

The statute makes no provision for instruction in the family. Compulsory primary education is framed exclusively around attendance at the enumerated establishments or, in the alternative, distance education, and no legal pathway exists by which parents may elect to educate a child at home.

A distinct situation has arisen in the two Anglophone regions, where armed separatist groups have, since 2017, imposed a boycott of state schooling and enforced it through attacks on schools, the killing and abduction of pupils and teachers, and the forced closure of establishments. UNICEF reported more than 855,000 children out of school in the Northwest and Southwest regions in November 2019. The disruption has persisted : reporting for 2023 estimated that some 1.4 million school-age children in Cameroon were in need of education assistance amid the separatist conflict in the west and Boko Haram-related insecurity in the north. The home-based and community learning that has emerged in this context consists of informal arrangements without basis in the national education statute.
