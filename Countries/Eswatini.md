# Unschooling in Eswatini

## Compulsory education

The 2005 Constitution (section 29(6)) gives every Swazi child the right to free education in public schools “at least up to the end of primary school, beginning with the first grade”. A second provision, section 60(8), says the State “shall promote free and compulsory basic education for all”, but it sits among the Directive Principles of State Policy ; under section 56(3), sections 57 to 63 are “not enforceable in any court or tribunal”. The education litigation has therefore rested on the enforceable right in section 29(6), with section 60(8) serving only as contextual support. The Grade 1–7 boundary is not in the Constitution ; it comes from the Free Primary Education Act, 2010, which defines “primary education” as instruction from Grade 1 up to and including Grade 7 (section 2).

Compulsion, where it exists, is statutory rather than constitutional. The Act places a duty on the parent of every child to whom it applies to cause the child “to receive education by regular attendance at a public primary school” (section 9(2)), enforced by the offence in section 10. This makes primary schooling compulsory in a narrow sense, from around age 6 to the end of Grade 7 (about ages 12–13). No compulsory education age is fixed beyond that point ; USDOL treats the framework as inadequate because it neither extends to age 15 nor guarantees nine years of free basic public education.

(The Act and the Constitution use the country’s former name, Swaziland ; it was renamed Eswatini in 2018.)

## Unschooling

Home education is not provided for. The duty under section 9(2) is discharged only by regular attendance at a public primary school, and the sole way out is to be excused under section 11, whose grounds are narrow : illness or danger of infection ; a physical or mental disability making ordinary instruction impossible ; temporary permission from the head teacher ; suspension without permission to enrol elsewhere ; or any other reason the head teacher accepts as good and sufficient. None of these is a home-education route. Failure to comply is an offence :

> _Offences_
>
> 10. (1) A parent of a child to whom this Act applies, who neglects or refuses to cause the child to attend school, unless the child is excused under section 11, commits an offence and is liable on conviction to a fine not exceeding E1,000 or in default of which, to three months community services.

Foreign pupils are treated differently, not identically : enrolment generally depends on the School Committee’s consent (section 4), although the Minister may declare classes of foreign pupils entitled to enrol as of right (section 5) ; foreign pupils are also subject to fees set by the Minister (section 6) unless exempted (section 7), and the government does not extend free-primary-education grants to non-citizens.

## News

The most recent assessment is USDOL’s *2024 Findings on the Worst Forms of Child Labor* (published September 2025), which rates Eswatini as having made “Minimal Advancement”. The headline domestic measure for the year was a 20% increase in funding for the Education Fund for Orphaned and Vulnerable Children, plus an additional US$1 million to cover school-related costs the fund did not ordinarily reach. The report again flags the gap created by the compulsory-schooling framework : because compulsory attendance effectively ends at Grade 7 (around ages 12–13), children who finish primary between ages 12 and 14 are neither required to be in school nor old enough to work legally (the minimum working age is 15). USDOL’s standing recommendation is to raise the compulsory education age to 15 and to establish by law nine years of free basic public education covering lower secondary, for all children, including non-citizens.
