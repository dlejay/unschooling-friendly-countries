# Unschooling in Armenia

## Compulsory education

Armenia provides general secondary education in a 12-year structure : elementary school (grades 1–4), middle school (grades 5–9), and high school (grades 10–12).
 
The **Law on General Education** (HO-160-N, 2009) draws the compulsory line at _basic general education_ (grades 1–9, roughly ages 6–15).
Article 4(4) : “The basic general education shall be compulsory, except for the cases prescribed by law”. Secondary education (grades 10–12) is stated as _free_ (Article 4(5)) but is not described as compulsory in the law's own terms. Children are admitted to grade 1 from the age of 6 (Article 16(2)).
 
The older **Law on Education** (1999, as amended) separately states that 12-year general secondary education, or primary/secondary vocational education, is compulsory until the learner turns 19 if that right has not been realised earlier (Article 18(7) : “The basic general education shall be mandatory”). This appears to describe the state's obligation to provide access rather than the parental enforcement age.
 
Parents are responsible for enrolling their school-age child in an educational institution (Law on General Education, Article 16(5)) and are liable for a child's frequent absence without good reason (Article 21(6)).

## Unschooling

Homeschooling is not available as a parental choice under Armenian law. The legislation creates a positive obligation to enrol in an institution and provides no opt-out mechanism for home-based education.
 
Key provisions of the Law on General Education :
 
- **Article 17(5) :** Home instruction exists only for children unable to attend school for health reasons. The list of qualifying diseases is defined by the Government.
- **Article 17(4) :** Individual learning may be organised under procedures established by the Ministry — this is a ministerially regulated mode, not a parental right.
- **Article 30(1)(19) :** The Ministry approves procedures for “individual instruction of learners, as well as external studies” — again, these are institutional modes under state authority.
- **Article 28(1)(1) :** Parents may “choose the educational institution and form of education for the instruction of a child”, but the law's structure makes clear this choice operates within the institutional framework, not outside it.
- **Article 16(5) :** Parents are responsible for enrolling their child in an educational institution.

## News
Armenia's current education policy strongly favours school inclusion, not home-based alternatives.
 
**Inclusive education rollout (August 2025).** Armenia's universal inclusive education system was to be introduced across the Republic by 1 August 2025. The Ministry of Education has stated that all schools are now implementing inclusive education, though parents and teachers have criticised the implementation as inadequate — citing insufficient infrastructure, trained staff, and individualised support.
 
**School governance centralisation (October 2025).** In October 2025, the National Assembly passed amendments to the Law on General Education centralising school governance. Schools currently managed by regional governors will transfer to the Ministry of Education, Science, Culture and Sports by September 2026, and those under the Yerevan Municipality by September 2027.
 
There are no widely visible Armenian advocacy groups campaigning for a general right to homeschool.
