# Unschooling in Venezuela

## Compulsory education

Article 102 of the Constitution of the Bolivarian Republic of Venezuela provides that education is a human right and a fundamental social duty, and that it is democratic, free and compulsory ; the State assumes education as an indeclinable function of maximum interest across all levels and modalities.

The current Ley Orgánica de Educación (LOE) was sanctioned on 13 August 2009 and published in Gaceta Oficial N.º 5.929 Extraordinario of 15 August 2009, and remains listed by the Asamblea Nacional as fully in force. The LOE declares education compulsory as a governing principle and organises the system around the basic-education subsystem, comprising educación inicial, educación primaria and educación media. Compulsory schooling has commonly been summarised as spanning ages 6 to 15, reflecting a nine-year basic-education model ; the LOE itself frames compulsory education by level rather than by age. Under Article 6, the State regulates, supervises and controls the obligatoriness of education and establishes mechanisms to require communities, families, parents, representatives and responsible adults to fulfil this social duty.

## Unschooling

Article 54 of the Ley Orgánica para la Protección de Niños, Niñas y Adolescentes (LOPNNA) provides that parents, representatives or responsible adults have the immediate obligation to guarantee a child's education and, consequently, must enrol them in a school, educational establishment or institute in accordance with the law, require their regular attendance at classes, and participate actively in their educational process. The provision is framed around school enrolment and attendance.

HSLDA argues that the Constitution and the wider human-rights framework provide a basis for home education : Article 76 establishes a shared and inalienable parental duty to raise, form, educate, maintain and assist children ; Article 59 protects freedom of religion and belief, including the right of parents to have their children receive religious education consistent with their convictions ; and Article 22 provides that the enumeration of constitutional rights is not to be read as denying other inherent rights. The LOE recognises families as co-responsible in education, with Article 17 assigning them a duty, right and responsibility in the orientation and formation of children in principles, values, beliefs, attitudes and habits. HSLDA does not cite a Venezuelan statute, regulation or domestic judicial decision authorising the practice.

Families who educate outside school reportedly certify studies through private or foreign "umbrella-school" arrangements.
