# Unschooling in Guam

## Compulsory education

A parent or guardian must ensure that a child who is at least 5 years old and has not yet reached 18 attends a public or private full-time day school, unless the child falls within a statutory exemption (17 GCA § 6102(a)).

The effective starting point for a five-year-old is governed by Guam's kindergarten-entry rules. In general, a child must be at least 5 years old by 31 July to enrol in kindergarten for that school year (§§ 6103 and 6105.1).

A student who graduates or obtains a GED before turning 18 is exempt from the upper-age requirement (§ 6102(b)).

## Home education

Home education operates under the private-instruction exemption in 17 GCA § 6109 :

> Children not attending a private full-time day school, and who are being instructed in study and recitation for at least three (3) hours a day for one hundred seventy (170) days each calendar year by a private tutor or other person in the several branches of study required to be taught in the public schools of Guam and in the English language, shall be exempted from attending the public schools.

The statute requires no prior notification, registration or approval, and imposes no teacher-qualification, testing, inspection or express record-keeping requirement. The U.S. Department of Education states that no territory policy currently exists concerning assessment or diplomas for homeschooled children, and HSLDA confirms that no paperwork or approval is required.

De jure, however, private instruction must take place for at least three hours a day on 170 days each calendar year, cover the several branches of study required in Guam's public schools, and be conducted in the English language.

Although the statute prescribes no curriculum or teaching method, the required breadth of subjects and minimum instructional time prevent education from being fully determined by the child's interests. Interest-led methods and projects remain possible within those constraints, but a wholly interest-driven education could omit legally required subjects — hence the "no" flag above.

The English-language requirement is also a significant constraint for families wishing to educate principally in another language.
