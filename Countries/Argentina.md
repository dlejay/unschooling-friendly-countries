# Unschooling in Argentina
## Compulsory education
Law N.º 26.206 (*Ley de Educación Nacional*, 2006) establishes that education is compulsory from the age of 4 until the completion of secondary school — approximately 14 years of mandatory schooling. The compulsory, free and secular character of Argentine primary education traces back to Law 1420 of 1884.
## Legal status of home education
Article 16 of Law 26.206 frames the duty as compulsory *schooling*, to be enforced by the Ministry of Education and the competent judicial authorities :
> School enforcement across the country extends from the age of four (4) years to the completion of the level of secondary education.
> The Ministry of Education and the competent judicial authorities shall ensure compliance with the obligation of school […].
The only home-based modality explicitly recognised in current law is *educación domiciliaria y hospitalaria* : a state-provided service for children who cannot attend ordinary school for health or other exceptional reasons. It is not voluntary parental home education, and it does not constitute a general statutory right to homeschool.
There is, accordingly, no general statutory right to elective homeschooling or unschooling under Argentine law. Some families educate at home in practice and rely on free-exam (*examen libre*) or alternative certification routes to obtain official credentials, but this practice is not a recognised general right and operates without dedicated legal protection.
## Statistics
Homeschooling networks (notably *Homeschooling Argentina*) estimate that 20 000 to 25 000 families were educating their children at home as of 2024. No official government figures exist. In the City of Buenos Aires, roughly 1 500 students per year register as free-exam candidates to obtain official certification.
## Proposed reform : *Ley de Libertad Educativa* (draft, not in force)
On 9 December 2025, the Milei government presented a 169-article draft bill — the *Ley de Libertad Educativa* — that would derogate Law 26.206 and, for the first time, grant home education an explicit legal framework. The bill was prepared by the Secretariat of Education under Carlos Torrendell within the *Consejo de Mayo*. The Government aimed to bring the bill to Congress from March 2026 ; as of 11 May 2026, it had not been enacted.
Provisions relevant to home education :
- **Article 49** defines *Educación en el hogar* as instruction directed by parents or legal guardians of children of compulsory-schooling age, delivered by them or by persons they designate.
- **Article 50** grants those providing the instruction free choice of methods, materials, rhythms and learning environments, subject to constitutional order and general law.
- **Article 51** requires registration in a provincial register through non-discretionary procedures, with no invasive controls on family life or on the pedagogical orientation chosen ; registers must be compatible with the *Base Nacional Homologada* of the SInIDE. Child-protection authorities retain access to registry data for rights-protection purposes.
- Knowledge acquisition is validated through standardised examinations on *contenidos mínimos comunes* defined by the *Consejo Federal de Educación* and provincial authorities. No teaching qualification is required for parents.
- No minimum age is set for entry into home education.
- *Educación en Entornos Virtuales de Aprendizaje* is recognised in parallel, including instruction by institutions based outside Argentina.
The broader reform also introduces voucher-like demand-side financing (vales, bonos, becas, créditos fiscales), a voluntary *Examen Nacional de Educación Secundaria*, publication of standardised test results by institution, the designation of education as an « essential service », and the removal of the 6 % of GDP financing target. The bill faces strong opposition from teacher unions and from several provincial governments, which retain key constitutional competencies in education.
