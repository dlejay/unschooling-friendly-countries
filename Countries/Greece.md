# Unschooling in Greece

## Compulsory schooling

Attendance in an accredited school is legally compulsory for roughly 11 years
(from ages ~4–5 through 14–15).

Article 2 of the _ΝΟΜΟΣ ΥΠ’ ΑΡΙΘ. 1566_ on the structure and function of primary and secondary education states :

> 3. Attendance is compulsory in elementary school and high school, as long as the student has not exceeded 16 years of age.
>    Anyone who has custody of a minor and fails to register or supervise their attendance is punished according to article 458 of the Criminal Code.

Home education is permitted only in narrowly defined exceptional cases — primarily serious health conditions or specific special-educational-needs determinations — and is always subject to state oversight, not parental choice.
The operative mechanism sits in **Article 6 §4.γ of Law 3699/2008**, which provides that where a pupil’s attendance is rendered impossible by serious short- or long-term health problems preventing mobility, their education may be delivered at home. Approval is granted by the competent Director of Education and requires a medical certificate from a public hospital ; pupils in this regime are classified as *«κατ’ ιδίαν διδαχθέντες»* and still sit examinations under standard rules. Delivery can be either in person or via τηλεκπαίδευση (distance learning). A narrow additional provision exists for foreign families resident in Greece where homeschooling is legal in their country of origin.

In mid-2024, a new education law (Law 5128/2024, Article 64) introduced technical adjustments to
these special provisions. It clarified that home teaching does not
always have to be delivered by a special-education teacher unless an official
assessment deems it necessary.
In other words, if a child is approved for home education due to health reasons, a regular teacher can be assigned except in cases where the state’s counseling center (ΚΕ.Δ.Α.Σ.Υ. / KESY) recommends a specialized educator.
This tweak aimed to streamline home-schooling for special cases, addressing complaints that the previous rule
(requiring special-ed certified teachers in all cases) was too rigid.
Aside from such technical clarifications, however, the law did not expand the eligibility for homeschooling
— it remains off-limits unless those strict criteria are met.

## News

**Custody case of a homeschooling mother (2015–2017):** In a widely reported case, child protective services intervened in 2015 to remove an
8-year-old child from her mother’s care because the mother refused to enroll her
in school.
The mother — herself a teacher — believed the Greek school system was ‘second-rate’ and wanted to personally homeschool
her daughter to provide a more hands-on education.
Authorities deemed this unlawful.
The Athens prosecutor’s office warned that if the child was not registered in a school,
the mother would lose custody — and police would even be sent to take the child.
In 2017 the case resurfaced when the mother was arrested in front of her child following an ‘abduction’ complaint filed by the father, and an Athens court sentenced her to 10 months’ imprisonment, suspended for 3 years.
Her lawyer, along with the human rights group ‘Pleiades’, criticized the draconian approach
(no formal hearing was held before the removal order) and argued the mother was acting in the child’s best interest.
This case — often cited by commentators — underscored the zero-tolerance enforcement of the anti-homeschool law.
It also prompted debate about parental rights : some observers sympathized with the mother’s desire for a better education, while others pointed out that Greek law leaves no room for personal choice in this matter.

**The ‘palaiochristianós’ case and the OIELE allegations (February 2024) :** In early 2024 Greek media reported on a father in the Corinthia region (nicknamed the ‘palaiochristianós’, meaning ‘old-Christian’) who kept his children out of school for religious reasons, the family reportedly living in caves in the mountainous interior.
This ‘off-the-grid’ homeschooling led to criminal prosecution — local authorities took strict action against the parents for violating compulsory education laws.
The term ‘φτωχοδιάβολοι της Κορινθίας’ (‘poor devils of Corinthia’) was used in press commentary to describe this family.

The Corinthia prosecution was the direct trigger for a public statement by the Union of Private School Teachers (**OIELE**, Ομοσπονδία Ιδιωτικών Εκπαιδευτικών Λειτουργών Ελλάδας), which restated and amplified findings it had first raised in 2017.
OIELE alleged that around **200–300 children** — mostly from affluent families linked to the *παραθρησκευτικός χώρος* (fringe / sectarian religious milieu), concentrated in wealthy Athens suburbs such as Glyfada and the northern suburbs — were being homeschooled off the books.
According to OIELE, these parents circumvent the law by enrolling their children in complicit private schools which issue paperwork and attendance records for students who never actually attend.
OIELE’s president described this as an underground ‘homeschooling with the help of private schools’ scheme, including the issuing of outright fake certificates (‘μαϊμού τίτλους’).
The union contrasted the severity of the prosecution of the Corinthia family with what it characterised as the state’s inaction toward equivalent cases in well-connected circles, and urged the Ministry of Education to investigate.

**Growth of the grey-zone workaround (2026 update) :** A March 2026 News24/7 investigation, relayed by Polis Media, reported that formal requests for κατ’ οίκον διδασκαλία routed through private schools for the 2024–25 school year were **more than double** those for the 2021–22 school year, and clearly higher than 2023–24 as well.
The investigation also documented a shift in composition : whereas such requests historically clustered around the final years of Lyceum, they now increasingly concern Gymnasio (middle-school) pupils. A recurring pattern flagged by the investigation is multiple children in the same family receiving identical medical diagnoses qualifying them for home instruction — consistent with OIELE’s long-standing allegation that the health-exemption route is being instrumentalised by specific (largely Athenian) family networks.
The legal framework itself is unchanged : as of the current review (April 2026), no broad crackdown or legislative response has materialised.
