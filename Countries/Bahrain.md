# Unschooling in Bahrain

## Compulsory education

The Constitution of the Kingdom of Bahrain, Article Seven (a), provides that the State sponsors educational and cultural services to its citizens, that education is compulsory and basic education free as specified and provided by law, and that the plan to combat illiteracy is laid down by law.

Law N°27 of 2005 regarding Education sets the compulsory-education framework. It defines the compulsory schooling age as beginning when a child reaches six (by the Gregorian date of birth) and ending at fifteen, and defines basic education (التعليم الأساسي) as the stage beginning at compulsory schooling age and lasting at least nine academic years, spanning the primary and intermediate cycles.

> Article Six
>
> Basic education is a right for children who reach the age of six at the beginning of the academic year, and the Kingdom is committed to providing it for them. Parents or guardians are obligated to implement this for a period of at least nine academic years. The Minister shall issue the necessary decisions to organize and implement this obligation for parents and guardians.

## Unschooling

The Article One definition of a school requires more than ten pupils taught by one or more teachers in formal instruction, and the compulsory-education provisions are framed around enrolment in education and attendance at school.

> Article Eight
>
> A fine not exceeding one hundred dinars shall be imposed on the parent of the child or the person in charge of him if he causes the child who has reached the compulsory age to fail to enroll in education, or to be absent from school without an acceptable excuse for a period of ten consecutive or non-consecutive days during the school year. Criminal proceedings shall not be initiated in either case except upon a request from the Ministry, and after it has warned the violator by a registered letter with acknowledgment of receipt.

The compulsory provisions — Article Six and Article Eight — refer to the child and to the parent or guardian without restriction to nationals ; the right to education in Article Two, by contrast, is guaranteed “to all citizens”, and the adult-literacy duty in Article Nine is confined to Bahrainis.
