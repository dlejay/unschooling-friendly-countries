# Unschooling in Haiti

## Compulsory education

Haiti’s Constitution guarantees the right to education and makes compulsory the level described in the French constitutional text as _l’enseignement primaire_ (Article 32-3) ; some English translations of the post-2011 amended text render this as “primary education”, while the standard rendering of the 1987 original is “primary schooling”.

The exact practical span of the compulsory obligation is not entirely clear from the Constitution alone. Haiti’s basic schooling is organised into three three-year cycles of _enseignement fondamental_, beginning around age 6. HSLDA currently lists Haiti’s compulsory education age as 6–11, while noting that its Haiti information is being updated. UNESCO PEER, by contrast, describes instruction as compulsory from 6 to 15, while also treating the first two cycles of fundamental education (up to around age 12) as the legally compulsory and free portion.

The safest reading is therefore : compulsory education clearly covers at least the primary / first fundamental cycles from around age 6, with some sources extending the obligation through basic education to age 15.

## Context

According to the Constitution (1987, as amended in 2011) :
> Article 32-3 :
>
> Primary schooling is compulsory under penalties to be prescribed by law. Classroom facilities and teaching materials shall be provided by the State to elementary school students free of charge.
There is no specific Haitian regulatory framework for home education. UNESCO PEER states that there does not appear to be regulation on _école à la maison_. Haiti is therefore best classified as a _de facto_ unregulated jurisdiction rather than one with an express homeschooling right or a formal registration procedure. In the absence of any known curriculum, inspection, approval, or testing regime applicable to home education, a family educating at home would appear able to start immediately and to follow an interest-driven approach in practice. This is a practical / legal-risk conclusion, not a clearly codified statutory right.

More broadly, independent sources describe Haitian education as a largely unregulated sector, with essentially no laws governing the setting up of schools and minimal oversight of non-public provision.
## Statistics
The 2023 World Bank country brief reports that more than 250,000 children aged 6–11 and about 65,000 youth aged 12–14 are not enrolled in school for financial or other reasons, and that roughly 80 % of primary schools are non-public.

Since 2023, the situation has deteriorated sharply. In a 28 February 2025 press statement, UNICEF reported that armed groups destroyed 47 schools in Port-au-Prince in January 2025, on top of 284 schools destroyed in 2024, and estimated that roughly one in seven children in Haiti was out of school, with close to one million more at risk of dropping out.
