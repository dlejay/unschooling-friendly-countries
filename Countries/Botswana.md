# Unschooling in Botswana

## Compulsory education
The Education Act (Cap. 58:01) does not itself impose compulsory school
attendance. Section 29(2)(d) confers on the Minister a regulation-making
power to prescribe compulsory attendance in any given area, with the
result that compulsion is contingent upon the exercise of that power
rather than established by the Act itself. No corresponding statutory
instrument is apparent on the face of the consolidated legislation.
Reference works including the TIMSS 2015 Encyclopedia, Nations
Encyclopedia and StateUniversity.com accordingly characterise Botswana’s
ten-year basic education — comprising seven years of primary schooling
from an entry age of six, followed by three years of junior secondary —
as not formally compulsory. Senior secondary education is neither free
nor compulsory.

Botswana’s report to UNESCO of 30 June 2024, submitted under the 1960
Convention and Recommendation against Discrimination in Education,
characterises primary and lower secondary education as compulsory and
free. That characterisation is not readily reconcilable with the text of
the Education Act or with the TIMSS account, save through implementing
regulations or some later legal instrument giving the policy effect ; no
such instrument is apparent.

## Home education
The Education Act contains no provision expressly authorising or
prohibiting home education. Its regulatory regime is directed at
"schools", a term defined as institutions or assemblies of not fewer than
ten pupils receiving regular instruction, whether by personal tuition or
correspondence, with institutions of a wholly or mainly religious
character expressly excluded. Section 14 renders the registration of
such schools compulsory. Single-family home education appears to fall
below the ten-pupil threshold and therefore does not appear to constitute
a registrable school within the meaning of the Act, although collective
arrangements involving ten or more pupils may engage the
school-registration regime.

No home-education registration scheme, curriculum-approval process,
inspection regime or standardised-testing requirement is apparent in the
legislation. The position is permissive but ambiguous, and has been
contested in practice : in May 2010 a magistrate’s court at Mahalapye is
reported by HSLDA to have ordered four families practising home
education to enrol their children in public school, invoking Botswana’s
obligations under United Nations human rights instruments.

## Empirical estimates
A figure of approximately three per cent of school-age children being
home educated is widely reproduced in the secondary literature, but
appears to derive from a single estimate of unverified provenance and is
not corroborated by any primary government source.
