# Unschooling in Saint Lucia

## Compulsory education

School attendance is compulsory from the beginning of the school year in which a child turns 5 until the end of the school year in which the child turns 15.

## Home education

Under the Education Act (Cap. 18.01), a parent may provide a home education programme if the parent complies with section 81 and the programme meets the statutory educational goals and objectives. The child must be registered with the Chief Education Officer before the programme begins and annually thereafter.

The parent must also prepare and provide an educational plan. The initial plan must be submitted **three months before** the home education programme begins. The plan must cover at least **three school years**, describe learning activities aligned with the statutory goals and objectives, and be based on the national curriculum established by the Minister.

A student enrolled in a home education programme is treated as excused from ordinary school attendance if the programme is in accordance with the Act.

The Chief Education Officer may assess the student’s achievement, advise the parent if the student is not making reasonable progress, and make recommendations. The Chief Education Officer must terminate the programme if it no longer meets the statutory requirements or if the student fails to meet achievement standards comparable to public-school students; the parent may appeal within 14 days.

Any parent or guardian residing in Saint Lucia — whether citizen or foreign national — is subject to the same rules.

## 2024 reform process

In 2024 the Ministry published a draft revised Education Bill and Regulations as part of a wider Education Act revision process. The Ministry page states that the 17 May 2024 draft Bill and Regulations were being shared after consultation and revision work.  [oai_citation:6‡Ministry of Education](https://www.education.gov.lc/2024/05/22/equip-education-act-2024)

The important point is that the core home-education requirements — prior registration, a three-month lead time, a three-year education plan, and alignment with the national curriculum — already appear in the existing Education Act, not only in the 2024 draft. The 2024 process should still be monitored, but it should not be described as the sole source of those requirements.
