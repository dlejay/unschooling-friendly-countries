# Unschooling in Connecticut

## Compulsory education

Conn. Gen. Stat. § 10‑184 sets compulsory education from age 5 to under 18. The lower bound was moved from 7 to 5 in 1998 (P.A. 98‑243) ; the upper bound was raised from 16 to 18 in 2001 (P.A. 00‑157).

Two opt‑outs at the lower end, exercised in person at the school district office : the parent of a 5‑year‑old may delay until age 6, and the parent of a 6‑year‑old may delay until age 7. At the upper end, a parent may consent to a 17‑year‑old's withdrawal before graduation by signing an in‑person withdrawal form.

Section 10‑185 sets a fine of up to $25 per day for non‑compliance, with a destitution exception.

## Unschooling

### According to CRHE

> **Alternative instruction provision**: Connecticut law exempts children receiving instruction "elsewhere" from compulsory school attendance. Parents must offer "equivalent instruction in the studies taught in the public schools." There are no notification, parent qualification, instruction time, bookkeeping, or assessment requirements.
> Connecticut's compulsory education statute includes an exemption when parents are "able to show that the children are elsewhere receiving equivalent instructions in the studies taught in the public schools." Connecticut has never passed a homeschool statute; from the earliest years of homeschooling to the present, parents have homeschooled under this "equivalent instruction" exemption. In 1982 the State Education Commissioner issued guidelines for school districts to follow when administering this exemption. New guidelines were issued in 1990, but neither set of rules ever had the force of law behind them, making them in effect voluntary. A 2002 bill to make the guidelines law failed to pass, as did a 2008 proposal.

### Further details

Section 10‑184 requires parents to be able to show, *if challenged*, that the child is receiving equivalent instruction. It does not create prior approval, annual assessment, parent‑qualification, instructional‑time, or routine reporting requirements. The State Department of Education presents notice, attendance logs, and portfolios as best‑practice guidance, not binding statewide conditions.

### Recent developments

#### May 2025 — Office of the Child Advocate report and Capitol rally

Over 2,000 people rallied at Connecticut's Capitol to protest potential regulations on homeschooling, while state officials cited cases of abuse and educational neglect linked to unregulated homeschooling. A report from the Office of the Child Advocate recommended oversight measures, noting that Connecticut was among the least regulated states for homeschooling. The report described Connecticut as one of only 12 states with "**no meaningful regulation of homeschooling.**" Republicans and homeschool advocates defended parental rights, arguing the focus should be on systemic failures in child protection, not homeschoolers.

### 2026 legislative session — H.B. 5468
Two child deaths drove a renewed push for legislation in the 2026 session : **Jacqueline "Mimi" Torres‑García** (11) of New Britain, whose body was discovered in autumn 2025, and **Eve Rogers** of Enfield. Both children had been withdrawn from public school to be homeschooled before their deaths.
Two vehicles emerged. The homeschooling provision in **S.B. 6** was stripped out on 14 April 2026 after the Connecticut State Department of Education raised concerns it would violate FERPA.
**H.B. 5468**, retitled *An Act Concerning the Provision of Parent‑Managed Learning* (the statement of purpose still refers to equivalent instruction ; "parent‑managed learning" is the new legislative framing), survived. After a rewrite that removed an earlier annual evidence‑of‑instruction requirement (and also dropped a provision that would have let homeschooled students take up to two public‑school classes and join extracurriculars), the bill passed the House 23 April 2026 along party lines, just before midnight. It is the first Connecticut homeschool oversight bill ever to clear a chamber.
As passed by the House, H.B. 5468 would :
- Require **all** Connecticut parents — not just those homeschooling — to indicate annually where they plan to send their child to school.
- Require parents withdrawing a child to homeschool to file an in‑person notice with the local school district.
- Trigger a one‑time check, when homeschooling begins, against the DCF abuse/neglect registry and open‑case list. If anyone in the household has an open DCF case or is on the registry, that family cannot homeschool. The bill explicitly states (line 180) that the records check does not constitute a DCF report.
The bill now heads to the **Senate**. As of 27 April 2026, the CGA records the latest action as File No. 724, Senate Calendar No. 461 — favorable report tabled for the Senate calendar, not final passage. Democratic appetite in the Senate has been described as tepid.
Current coverage routinely describes Connecticut as the only US state without laws governing homeschooling. Strictly, § 10‑184 does govern home education through its "elsewhere receiving equivalent instruction" exemption ; the more precise claim is that Connecticut has no *dedicated* homeschool statute imposing routine notice, approval, assessment, or reporting.
