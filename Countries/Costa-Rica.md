# Unschooling in Costa Rica

## Compulsory education

By law, preschool, general básica and diversificada levels are all obligatory.
In practice this means roughly **13 years of schooling**:
two years of pre-primary (ages ~4–5), six years of primary (Grades 1–6, I and II ciclos, ages ~6–11),
three years of lower secondary (Grades 7–9, III ciclo, ages ~12–14),
and two years of upper secondary (Grades 10–11, educación diversificada, ages ~15–16).
Children are expected to be enrolled from age four (pre-primary) up to roughly age 17
under Article 78 of the _Constitución Política_ (as reformed in 2011),
Article 59 of the _Código de la Niñez y la Adolescencia_,
and the _Ley Fundamental de Educación_ (Ley 2160).

## Home education

There is no legal basis for home education in Costa Rica.
The Ministry of Public Education (MEP) does not recognise homeschooling as a valid way
to discharge the compulsory-schooling obligation, and families who homeschool
do so without formal state supervision or certification.

Article 78 of the _Constitución Política_ and Article 59 of the _Código de la Niñez y la Adolescencia_
reinforce each other on this point. Article 59 reads:

> Preschool education, basic general education and diversified education will be free,
> compulsory and paid for by the State.

## News

In October 2024, a new bill — **Expediente N.° 24.648,
_Ley para la Regulación de la Educación a Distancia y en Casa (Homeschooling) en Costa Rica_** —
was introduced in the Asamblea Legislativa to formally legalise
and regulate home and distance education.
The proposal amends the _Ley Fundamental de Educación_ and the _Código de la Niñez_
to recognise _educación en casa_ as a valid modality for discharging the right to education,
alongside in-person and distance learning, under MEP supervision.

In March 2025 the bill was assigned to a special commission
(reference AL-CE23169) and sent out for mandatory consultation to the public universities
and other institutional stakeholders. The responses received between April and August 2025
were substantively critical rather than supportive:

- The **Instituto Tecnológico de Costa Rica** (TEC) concluded that the bill,
  while not directly encroaching on the TEC's remit,
  could collide with the constitutional autonomy of public universities
  in the regulation of distance education (constitutional Articles 84 and 88).
- The **Universidad Nacional** (UNA), through its CIDE and Psychology school,
  highlighted that homeschooling is already practised in Costa Rica
  _"without legal backing or state supervision,_
  _and frequently promoted by conservative, religious, anti-vaccine or foreign groups,"_
  raising child-rights concerns.
- The **Universidad de Costa Rica** (UCR) similarly flagged autonomy issues.

As of the most recent publicly traceable activity (mid-to-late 2025),
the bill remains in consultation / committee phase and has not been reported out for plenary debate.
No dictamen — favourable or negative — has been issued.
