# Unschooling in Zimbabwe

## Compulsory education

The Education Act [Chapter 25:04], as amended by the Education Amendment Act, 2019 (Act 15 of 2019, in force 6 March 2020), defines "basic education" as education from early childhood development (ECD) up to Form Four, and "basic state-funded education" as education from ECD up to Form Four, adult education up to Form Four, or any further category the Minister may declare by notice in the Gazette.
The right is expressed subject to section 75 of the Constitution.

The school system is structured as two years of ECD, seven years of primary school (Grades 1-7), four years of lower secondary (Forms 1-4), and two years of upper secondary (Forms 5-6).
The legal entitlement is framed as basic state-funded education through Form Four ; in practice, households may still face fees and levies, especially at secondary level, and enrolment falls after the Grade 7 examination.

The amendment substituted section 5 :

> 5 Compulsory education
>
> (1) Every child shall be entitled to compulsory basic state funded education.
>
> (2) Any parent who deprives their child the right to basic state funded education shall be guilty of an offence and liable to a fine not exceeding level 6 or to imprisonment for a period not exceeding two years.

## Home education

Home education is available in practice, most visibly through the Zimbabwe Government Primary Correspondence School, a State distance-education programme that describes itself as a homeschooling route.
It admits primary-level learners through Grade 7, accepts learners inside and outside Zimbabwe, and allows a learner to start at any time of year and progress at their own pace.
It provides the primary subjects prescribed by the Curriculum Development Unit (CDU), and home-schooled Grade 7 learners sit the ZIMSEC national examination.
Private and online curricula (e.g. Cambridge) are also used.

No separate statutory home-education approval regime was found, and no provision expressly requiring home educators outside the correspondence-school route to follow the official curriculum was found.
Section 5 penalises depriving a child of basic state-funded education and does not prescribe a home curriculum.
No instance of adverse State action against home-educating or unschooling families was found, and no Zimbabwean account of curriculum-free, interest-driven home education was located.

## Out-of-school context

UNICEF reports that about nine in ten primary-age children are in school, and that around half of adolescents aged 13 to 19 are not in school.
A 2022 report cited by VOA put the out-of-school share of youth at 47%, against 21% before the COVID-19 pandemic, attributing the rise to poverty.
The academic literature estimates Zimbabwe's out-of-school population at nearly one million children.
