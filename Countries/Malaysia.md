# Unschooling in Malaysia

## Compulsory education

Under current law, primary education (Tahun 1–6) has been compulsory since 2003 (six years).
Compulsory secondary education has been legislated for but is not yet in force : the Education (Amendment) Act 2025, passed by the Dewan Rakyat on 30 July 2025, extends the statutory definition of "compulsory education" to cover both primary and secondary levels.
The Act comes into operation only on a date appointed by the Minister by notification in the Gazette, and the secondary duty itself takes effect once the Minister prescribes it by gazette order under the new s.32A(1).
The gazetted commencement and prescription status should be verified before relying on enforcement.

## Home education
Under the 2025 amendment, once commenced, "compulsory education" will mean primary education prescribed under subsection 29A(1) and secondary education prescribed under subsection 32A(1).
The original (pre-2025) primary-education provision reads :
> Compulsory primary education
>
> 29A. (1) The Minister may, by order published in the Gazette,
> prescribe primary education to be compulsory education.
>
> (2) Every parent who is a Malaysian citizen residing in Malaysia shall ensure that if his child has attained
> the age of six years on the first day of January of the current school year that child is enrolled as a pupil in a primary school
> in that year and remains a pupil in a primary school for the duration of the compulsory education.
The 2025 amendment changes the legal trigger : the duty will no longer attach to "every parent who is a Malaysian citizen residing in Malaysia", but to every parent whose child is a Malaysian citizen and resides in Malaysia. A parallel obligation for secondary education will be inserted under the new s.32A once the amendment is commenced, with the same enforcement architecture. Non-compliance with the primary obligation, and with the secondary obligation once in force, may result in a fine of up to RM 5 000, imprisonment of up to six months, or both.
In practice, Malaysian parents (of children meeting the citizenship-and-residence test) must enrol their six-year-olds in Year 1 and ensure they complete Years 1–6. Once the 2025 Act is commenced and the Minister prescribes secondary education as compulsory under s.32A(1), the duty is expected to extend for the duration of secondary education.
Officially, homeschooling remains legal but tightly regulated. It is treated as a discretionary exemption from the compulsory-education requirement under the Minister's power in subsection 29A(3) for primary level — and, under the new s.32A(3), once in force, for secondary level. Homeschooling is not a right : the KPM (Kementerian Pendidikan Malaysia / MOE) treats it as a privilege, the approval is curriculum-bound (a recognised national or international syllabus must be followed) and contingent on ministerial decision. Applications (_borang_) are processed through the State Education Department (JPN) and KPM, and are subject to ministerial approval. KPM describes the usual exemption categories as chronic health conditions, gifted children, and families who travel frequently.
Note that attendance at registered international, private, or Chinese independent schools is treated as schooling, not homeschooling ; it does not use the homeschooling exemption route.

## News

1. Jul 2025 : Education (Amendment) Bill 2025 tabled (28 Jul) and passed by the Dewan Rakyat (30 Jul). It will substitute the definition of "compulsory education" in section 2 of Act 550, once commenced, to encompass both primary and secondary levels ; will change the parental duty trigger to parents whose child is a Malaysian citizen and resides in Malaysia ; and will insert a new s.32A empowering the Minister, once the provision is commenced, to prescribe secondary education as compulsory by Gazette order. Penalties for non-compliance : up to RM 5 000, six months' imprisonment, or both.
2. Commencement and prescription : the 2025 Act takes effect only on a date appointed by the Minister by notification in the Gazette, and the secondary duty itself only bites once the Minister has additionally prescribed secondary education as compulsory under s.32A(1). Verify both steps before relying on enforcement.
3. Homeschooling : no dedicated reform. The exemption mechanism remains the Minister's discretionary power under s.29A(3) for primary, replicated under s.32A(3) for secondary once in force. The KPM continues to treat home education as a discretionary, curriculum-bound privilege rather than a right.
