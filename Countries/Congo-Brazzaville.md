# Unschooling in the Republic of the Congo

## Compulsory education

Article 29 of the Constitution of 25 October 2015 provides that the State ensures the
development of youth and, to that end, guarantees notably the right to education, equal
access to teaching and training, and compulsory schooling until the age of 16.
The Constitution does not state a starting age; the texts consulted do not expressly fix
a general one. The range of 6 to 16 appears in Article 3 of the 2019 home-education
decree, as the age condition for that exceptional regime.

## Home education

Article 5 of the _Loi n° 25-95 du 17 novembre 1995_, which reorganised the education
system and remains in force, provides that education is delivered in public and private
institutions, then adds :

> Exceptionally, teaching may be provided in the family, under conditions laid down by
> decree adopted in the _Conseil des ministres_.

Those conditions are laid down by the _Décret n° 2019-441 du 30 décembre 2019 fixant les
conditions d’organisation de l’enseignement dans la famille_, published in the _Journal
officiel_ of 23 January 2020. The decree defines home education as a temporary
arrangement intended for two categories (Article 2) :

- Congolese children settled abroad where there is no Congolese consular school or
  school of Francophone culture ;
- Congolese or foreign children residing in localities of Congo lacking schools and
  teachers.

Under Article 3, a child of compulsory-instruction age, between 6 and 16, residing in a
foreign country without a Congolese consular school, may benefit from home education.
A Congolese child living on national territory is ordinarily excluded : home education is
permitted only in a locality wholly lacking schools, by decision of the Minister in
charge of education, following a reasoned opinion from the departmental prefect.
(Articles 2 and 3 do not use identical wording : Article 2 refers to the absence of a
consular school _or_ a school of Francophone culture and covers Congolese or foreign
children within Congo, while Article 3 mentions only the consular school and, for the
domestic exclusion, only "the Congolese child". The regime is narrow on any reading.)

Teaching may take place outside the child’s home but may group only children of the same
household. It is provided by the parents themselves, though the family may entrust it to
a person with the required competence (Article 4).

## Procedure

Parents abroad must declare their intention to the Congolese embassy in the host State
one month before the start of the school year (Article 5). Parents in an eligible
locality within Congo must submit a written declaration to the departmental primary and
secondary education inspectorate, stating the child’s identity, the identity,
qualifications and diplomas of the parents and of any person in charge of the teaching,
and the address where teaching takes place (Article 6). For residents of Congo, the
declaration does not by itself create eligibility : the ministerial decision under
Article 3 is also required.

For children abroad, the ambassador or cultural adviser conducts an inquiry from the
first year, renewed every two years until the child turns 16. It concerns the reasons
for choosing this mode of instruction and its compatibility with the child’s health and
living conditions, and is expressly distinct from the pedagogical control (Article 7).

## Curriculum and methods

The programmes and prescribed content of home education are fixed by order of the
Minister in charge of primary and secondary education (Article 8). The teaching need not
follow conventional school methods : the family freely chooses the means and methods for
reaching the level of those programmes, but this choice must first be submitted for the
assessment of the cultural adviser (families abroad) or of the departmental director or
a pedagogical inspector (eligible families within Congo).

No ministerial order fixing these programmes was located in the published official
sources consulted. Pedagogical flexibility under Article 8 concerns the means of
delivering the official curriculum ; it does not permit replacing that curriculum with
interest-led content.

## Inspection

A pedagogical inspector mandated by the ministry checks each child individually at least
once a year ; the family receives written notice of the date and place at least one month
in advance, and remote evaluation may be used (Article 9). If the results are judged
insufficient, a second check takes place no sooner than one month after the first
results are sent, to give the family time to improve the child’s schooling situation
(Article 10). If the second check is still insufficient, the parents are ordered to
enrol the child in a school ; deliberate refusal exposes them to sanctions under the
applicable regulations (Articles 11 and 12). State end-of-cycle examinations remain
organised by the direction des examens et concours (Article 13).

## Pending reform

On 20 January 2026, the _Conseil des ministres_ approved a _projet de loi fixant
l’organisation du système éducatif en République du Congo_, intended to replace the
_Loi n° 25-95_ and to be transmitted to Parliament. As of the last review, no enacted
replacement law was located in the official sources consulted ; the 1995 law and the
2019 decree are therefore treated here as the applicable published framework.
