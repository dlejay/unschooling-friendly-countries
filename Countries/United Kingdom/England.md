# Unschooling in England

## Compulsory education

Under section 7 of the Education Act 1996, parents must ensure that every
child of compulsory school age receives an efficient full-time education
suitable to:

- their age, ability and aptitude; and
- any special educational needs they may have,

either through regular attendance at school or otherwise.

Compulsory school age begins on 31 December, 31 March or 31 August following
the child's fifth birthday, whichever comes first. It ends on the last Friday
in June in the school year in which the child turns 16.

After reaching school-leaving age, young people in England must continue in
education or training until they are 18. This does not require them to remain
at school.

## Home education

Elective home education is lawful throughout England. Parents do not need to
be qualified teachers and do not normally need the permission of the local
authority.

Where a child has never been registered at a school, the parents are currently
under no general legal obligation to register the child as home educated or
obtain prior approval from the local authority.

Where a child attends an ordinary mainstream school, the parent should notify
the school in writing that the child will cease attending after a specified
date and will receive education otherwise than at school. Once that date has
passed, the school must remove the child's name from its admission register and
inform the local authority.

Prior consent or another legal step may nevertheless be required where:

- the child attends a special school under arrangements made by a local
  authority; or
- a school attendance order is in force.

Home education should begin without a significant gap after the child leaves
school.

## Unschooling

“Unschooling” is not a term defined in English law. However, an informal,
child-led or interest-driven education can be lawful.

Home-educating parents are not legally required to:

- follow the National Curriculum;
- reproduce school hours, terms or timetables;
- provide formal lessons;
- use detailed lesson plans;
- enter the child for particular qualifications;
- formally mark work or assess progress; or
- reproduce school-style peer-group socialisation.

Nevertheless, the education as a whole must remain efficient, full-time and
suitable to the individual child's age, ability, aptitude and special
educational needs. A wholly passive arrangement in which no meaningful
education is being provided would not satisfy this requirement.

There is no fixed statutory number of hours for home education. Its full-time
nature is assessed according to the circumstances of the individual child.

## Local-authority oversight

Local authorities do not currently have a general statutory duty to conduct
routine inspections or monitoring visits of every home-educated child.

They do, however, have duties to identify children in their area who may not
be receiving a suitable education. A local authority may therefore make
informal enquiries after becoming aware that a child is being home educated.

Parents are not ordinarily required by education law to accept a home visit or
respond to an informal enquiry. However, failing to provide sufficient
information may lead the authority to conclude that it appears the child is
not receiving a suitable education.

The authority may then issue a notice under section 437(1) of the Education
Act 1996. If the parents do not satisfy it that suitable education is being
provided, the authority may pursue a school attendance order.

## Children’s Wellbeing and Schools Act 2026

The Children’s Wellbeing and Schools Act 2026 received Royal Assent on
29 April 2026.

It contains substantial reforms relating to children who are not registered
at school, including:

- compulsory local-authority registers of children not in school;
- duties on parents and certain education providers to supply information;
- more systematic consideration of whether registered children are receiving
  suitable education;
- a requirement for local-authority consent before certain vulnerable
  children, and certain children attending special schools, can be withdrawn
  for home education; and
- a pilot scheme for mandatory pre-withdrawal meetings in a limited proportion
  of local-authority areas.

As of 24 July 2026, these Children Not in School measures are not yet in force.
The current home-education arrangements therefore remain applicable.

The Department for Education has stated that implementation will not be
immediate. Consultation on regulations and statutory guidance, parliamentary
approval of relevant secondary legislation, publication of final guidance and
a pre-implementation period are expected before the new framework commences.

## Statistics

The following figures record children of compulsory school age who were known
to English local authorities as electively home educated on the autumn census
date:

| Autumn census | Children known to be home educated |
| ------------- | -----------------------------------: |
| 2025          |                              126,000 |
| 2024          |                              111,700 |
| 2023          |                      92,000 estimated |

The 2023 figure was adjusted for local-authority non-response. The national
data collection became mandatory in autumn 2024, and the Department for
Education cautions that part of the recorded increase may reflect improvements
in reporting and data quality.

These are point-in-time figures. They do not represent the total number of
children who experienced home education at any point during the entire
academic year.
